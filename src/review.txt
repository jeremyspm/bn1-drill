CELLDIV-1 | Skin repairs itself by mitosis. What are the two steps of mitosis, and what is the phase called when a cell is NOT dividing?
    => Mitosis = karyokinesis (the nucleus/genetic material divides) then cytokinesis (the cytoplasm splits) — giving two identical daughter cells. A non-dividing cell is in INTERPHASE, when it grows and copies its DNA ready for the next division.
    why: Interphase is the longest part of the cell cycle.

CELLDIV-2 | Descriptions: Which description goes with which diagram? Metaphase: The chromosomes line up across the middle (equator)of the spindle fibres Telophase: A new nuclear membrane starts to form around each set of chromatids, and the cytoplasm divides across the middle forming two new cells, with the same number of chromosomes. Anaphase: The centromeres holding the chromatids together break and the chromatids move to opposite ends (spindle poles) of the cell. Prophase: The chromatin is coiled up to show chromosomes as a pair of chromatids. The centrioles start to form spindle fibres for the chromatids to move along.
    => Stages of mitosis: PROPHASE — chromatin coils into visible chromosomes (paired chromatids); centrioles form spindle fibres. METAPHASE — chromosomes line up on the middle (equator) of the spindle. ANAPHASE — centromeres split; chromatids pulled to opposite poles. TELOPHASE — new nuclear membranes form around each set; cytoplasm divides (cytokinesis) into two identical cells.

CELLDIV-3 | In mitosis, name the stage where chromatids are (a) lined up on the spindle equator, and (b) being pulled to opposite poles. Then list the four stages in order.
    => (a) On the equator = METAPHASE. (b) Moving to the poles = ANAPHASE. Order: Prophase, Metaphase, Anaphase, Telophase.
    why: Mnemonic PMAT: 'Met in the Middle, pulled Apart in Anaphase.'

CELLDIV-4 | Give the single identifying feature that tells each mitosis stage apart: prophase, metaphase, anaphase, telophase.
    => Prophase = chromosomes condense and become visible, no equator line yet. Metaphase = chromosomes lined up single-file on the equator. Anaphase = sister chromatids split and move to opposite poles. Telophase = two new nuclei reform and the cytoplasm divides (cytokinesis).
    why: Look for the equator line: on it = metaphase, split from it = anaphase.

CELLDIV-5 | Contrast mitosis and meiosis on four points: where it happens, number of daughter cells, chromosome number of the daughters, and its purpose.
    => MITOSIS: happens in all body tissues; 2 daughter cells; full chromosome number (diploid, 46 in humans); for growth and repair. MEIOSIS: only in ovaries/testes; 4 daughter cells; HALF the chromosomes (haploid, 23); makes gametes for sexual reproduction ('reduction division').
    why: Halving in meiosis means fertilisation restores 46 (23 + 23).

TIS1-1 | Name the layers of the epidermis from deepest to most superficial. Which layer divides, and which is dead? Then: what tissue makes up the dermis and the hypodermis?
    => Epidermis deep to superficial: stratum BASALE (dividing stem cells) - SPINOSUM - GRANULOSUM - LUCIDUM (thick skin only) - CORNEUM (dead, keratin-filled, waterproof surface). The dermis = dense IRREGULAR connective tissue. The hypodermis = mostly ADIPOSE (fat) tissue.
    why: Only the basale divides; cells flatten, fill with keratin and die as they rise.

TIS1-2 | Why is the epidermis classed as 'stratified squamous' epithelium, and where in it do you find the melanocytes and the dividing cells?
    => Stratified = many cell layers; squamous = the surface cells are flat — so 'stratified squamous'. The melanocytes (make melanin for UV protection) and the dividing stem cells both sit in the deepest layer, the stratum basale.
    why: As cells are pushed up they flatten, keratinise and die.

TIS1-3 | Dense irregular connective tissue: where is it in the skin, how are its collagen fibres arranged, and why does that suit the dermis?
    => Found in the DERMIS (deep to the epidermis). Its collagen fibres run in many directions (criss-crossed / irregular), so it resists pulling forces from ALL directions — ideal for skin that gets stretched every way.
    why: Contrast dense REGULAR (parallel fibres) in tendons — strong in one direction only.

TIS1-4 | Loose (areolar) connective tissue: how do you recognise it, where is it found, and what does it do?
    => Looks loose and open — sparse cells and scattered collagen + elastic fibres in a gel matrix, with fibroblasts. Found under epithelia, around vessels and organs, and in the hypodermis. It holds structures in place, cushions them, and is the main site of inflammation and immune defence.
    why: 'Areolar' = little open spaces.

TIS1-5 | Adipose tissue: how do you recognise it under the microscope, where is the fat stored in each cell, and what are its roles besides storing energy?
    => Cells look like empty rings ('signet-ring'): one big fat droplet fills the cell and pushes the nucleus and cytoplasm to the rim. Besides energy storage it provides insulation (heat), cushioning/protection of organs, and acts as an endocrine tissue (e.g. releases leptin).
    why: The 'empty' look is because the fat washes out during slide prep.

TIS1-6 | Which muscle type is the arrector pili (the skin muscle that raises hairs / causes goosebumps), and what does it do?
    => SMOOTH muscle (involuntary). It contracts to pull the hair upright and dimple the skin ('goosebumps'), and helps squeeze sebum out of the sebaceous glands.
    why: Smooth muscle: spindle-shaped fibres, one central nucleus, no stripes/striations.

TIS1-7 | Blood is a connective tissue. Name the granulocytes and the agranulocytes with one function each, and say why blood counts as a connective tissue.
    => Granulocytes: NEUTROPHILS (first responders, engulf bacteria), EOSINOPHILS (parasites and allergy), BASOPHILS (release histamine for inflammation). Agranulocytes: LYMPHOCYTES (B cells make antibodies, T cells kill infected cells), MONOCYTES (become macrophages). Blood is a connective tissue because it is cells suspended in a large extracellular MATRIX (plasma).
    why: 'Never Let Monkeys Eat Bananas' — neutrophils, lymphocytes, monocytes, eosinophils, basophils by frequency.

TIS1-8 | Name the parts of a neuron and their jobs, say what the neuroglia do, and state what is damaged in multiple sclerosis (MS).
    => CELL BODY (holds the nucleus, integrates signals) - DENDRITES (receive incoming signals) - AXON (carries the impulse away), usually wrapped in a MYELIN sheath that speeds conduction. Neuroglia are support cells (astrocytes support/feed, oligodendrocytes make myelin, microglia = immune defence, ependymal make CSF). In MS the MYELIN is destroyed, so impulse conduction is impaired.
    why: Grey matter = cell bodies; white matter = myelinated axons.

TIS2-18 | Connective-tissue fibre arrangement: how do dense regular, dense irregular and loose connective tissue differ, and give an example of each?

    why: Only viruses are non-cellular and can't reproduce on their own.

    why: This is why antibiotics (which target bacterial cells) don't work on viruses.

PH-1 | What does the pH scale measure, which ion does it reflect, what is its range, and which value is neutral?
    => pH measures how ACIDIC or alkaline a solution is — specifically the concentration of HYDROGEN (H+) ions. The scale runs 0 to 14; 7 is NEUTRAL; below 7 is acidic (more H+); above 7 is alkaline/basic (fewer H+).
    why: Each 1 pH step = a 10-fold change in H+ concentration.

PH-2 | Give the approximate pH ranges for a strong acid, a weak acid, neutral, a weak base and a strong base. What is blood's normal pH, and what values define acidosis and alkalosis?
    => Strong acid ~0–2 (battery acid, gastric acid); weak acid ~5–6; neutral 7 (saliva); weak base ~7–8; strong base ~13–14 (drain cleaner). Blood is normally slightly alkaline, ~7.35–7.45. Acidosis = blood pH below 7.35; alkalosis = above 7.45.
    why: Blood's safe range is tiny — only about 7.35 to 7.45.
